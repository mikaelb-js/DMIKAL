Drum Machines I've Known and Loved: A look at a few Roland models

In this overview we're looking at a few of the drum machines I've used and appreciated in the studio myself and its use by other artists, that many times have resulted in music I've enjoyed listening to.

Roland TR-909

Released in 1983, the TR-909 after larglely having flopped, slowly became to be one of the most influential drum machines in electronic music. Its sound is defined by punchy, analog bass drums, sharp snares, and its distinctive hi-hat and cymbal sounds, which are a mix of analog and digital elements. The 909 is famously used in genres like techno, acid house as well as early hip hop, thanks to its possibilities for iconic heavy, driving rhythms. Pioneers like Daft Punk, The Chemical Brothers, and Aphex Twin embraced and both used and abused this legendary model, helping to define the sounds of 90s dance music. The machine’s sequencer is especially loved for its ease of use as well as its imperfections making it a go-to for live performances and studio tracks alike.

Roland TR-707 Rhythm Composer

Launched in 1985, the TR-707 was a digital drum machine known for its crisp, clear drum sounds. It featured a set of samples for drums like kick, snare, and toms, with notable claps and cymbals that gave it a bright, synthetic flavor. Artists like New Order and Bomb the Bass utilized the TR-707, contributing to its popularity in synthpop, new wave, and early house music. Though its sounds were digital, it had a distinctive warmth that made it a versatile instrument for both electronic and pop genres. The machine's straightforward programming and 12-bit sample resolution gave it a unique place in the history of drum machines.

Designed by Roland’s engineering team during the mid-80s, the TR-707 was meant to be a more affordable option for musicians . It was one of the first drum machines to feature an LCD screen and a sequencer with a visual interface, which made it more user-friendly than many of its contemporaries. Despite its less groundbreaking sound, it became a favorite in the later burgeoning house and techno scenes.

Artists and Musical Effect

Artists such as The Orb and Bomb the Bass used the 707 to craft their early acid and ambient house tracks. Its digital nature and tight, punchy sounds made it a staple for electronic music producers during the 80s and early 90s. Tracks like Bomb the Bass – Beat Dis and The Orb – Little Fluffy Clouds showcase the 707’s ability to create solid, driving rhythms with a bit of grit.

Music examples

  • New Order - "Blue Monday"

Roland R-8 Human Rhythm Composer

The Roland R-8, released in 1989, is notable for its combination of analog and PCM sounds, giving it a more organic,"human" feel compared to its predecessors. It offered multiple layers of customization, allowing users to adjust drum sounds in depth—creating a wide palette from realistic acoustic drum kits to synthetic percussion. Artists like The Orb and Leftfield were early adopters of the R-8, using it for its ability to craft deep, rhythmic textures in ambient, techno, and house music. One standout feature of the R-8 is its "Humanizer" function, which randomize timing and velocity to give patterns a more natural swing. This made it ideal for creating groove-heavy, nuanced beats that stood out from the purely mechanical feel of earlier machines.

Creators and Legacy

The R-8 was developed by Roland’s engineers in response to the demand for a drum machine with both realism and flexibility in its programming. The machine was particularly popular among producers in the early days of the acid house and industrial music scenes due to its broad sound palette and ability to produce intricate rhythms. Although it didn’t come to achieve the same iconic status as the TR-909, it found a loyal user base among producers who appreciated its versatility.

Artists and Musical Effect

The R-8’s ability to blend natural and electronic drum sounds made it a favorite of industrial pioneers like Front 242 and Skinny Puppy. Its extensive onboard sounds and user-programmable options helped create complex, evolving rhythms in genres ranging from industrial to early hip-hop. Artists like The Prodigy and even J Dilla experimented with the R-8 to shape the gritty, bass-heavy sounds of their productions.

Music examples

  • The Prodigy – Voodoo People
  • Skinny Puppy - Assimilate

Roland TR-727 Rhythm Composer

Released in 1985, the TR-727 was a variant of the TR-707, designed specifically for Latin percussion. It featured a collection of high-quality samples of congas, bongos, timbales, and other percussion instruments, which made it ideal for adding ethnic rhythms to pop, dance, and even jazz music. With a distinct digital sound that still carries warmth, the TR-727 was favored by artists such as Santana and Herbie Hancock for its ability to blend traditional Latin sounds with electronic music production. Its sequencer was also simple yet powerful, allowing for intricate rhythm programming that could seamlessly integrate into tracks. Though it was often overlooked compared to the TR-808 and TR-909, the 727 remains a beloved choice for producers seeking unique percussion textures.

Creators and Legacy

Also released in 1985, the TR-727 was part of Roland's attempt to diversify its drum machine offerings, targeting genres such as Latin, pop, and dance. The machine was designed with a similar interface to the TR-808 and TR-909 but focused more on rhythmic elements from Latin and world music. Despite being overshadowed by the popularity of the TR-808 and TR-909, the TR-727 gained a niche following among musicians experimenting with Latin and world music rhythms.

Artists and Musical Effect

Artists like M.I.A. and The Beastie Boys incorporated the TR-727’s Latin percussion sounds into their tracks. M.I.A.'s use of world rhythms in Paper Planes is a prime example of how the TR-727's sounds can influence contemporary music. The machine’s distinctive percussion samples were also used by hip-hop producers looking for a different flavor in their beats.

Technical Underpinnings: What’s Inside Each Drum Machine?

  • TR-909

    The TR-909 is a hybrid machine, combining analog synthesis for bass drum, snare, toms, and cymbals with digital sampling for hi-hats and cymbals. It features a step sequencer that allows for 16 steps per pattern, making it easy to create repeating rhythmic loops. The 909's analog sound is created through voltage-controlled oscillators (VCOs) for the bass drum and snare, giving it its signature deep punch and sharp snap. The digital parts were sampled at 12-bit resolution, giving it a unique crunchy character, especially on the hi-hats and cymbals. Its ability to sync with other Roland gear using MIDI made it an industry standard for dance music producers.

  • TR-707

    The TR-707 uses digital samples stored on ROM chips, each sound representing a different drum or percussion instrument. With 12-bit resolution, the samples have a clear, crisp, and somewhat synthetic character, offering a clean sound that defined the '80s electronic music landscape. The machine’s sequencer operates on a 16-step loop pattern, and it offers easy-to-use programming with options for shuffle and accent to adjust rhythm. It lacks the analog warmth of the TR-909 but compensates with its precise and customizable sounds. The TR-707 does not feature real-time pitch modulation or synthesis, but its simplicity and solid build made it a reliable tool for live performance and studio work.

  • R-8

    The R-8 is a digital drum machine with both PCM (sample-based) and analog-sounding options. It features a powerful sequencer capable of creating intricate patterns with variable tempo and swing, and it allows users to adjust individual drum sounds' pitch, tone, and decay. It has a 12-bit resolution, similar to the TR-707, but with greater depth in sound editing. The R-8’s standout feature is its "Humanizer" function, which can introduce slight timing and velocity variations to emulate human imperfection in drumming. This ability to customize both sounds and sequencing makes it an excellent choice for those looking for flexibility in their rhythmic composition.

  • TR-727

    The TR-727 is a digital drum machine with ROM samples, specifically designed for Latin percussion.It contains 15 percussion sounds, each sampled at 12-bit resolution, including congas, bongos, timbales, and cymbals. The machine uses a simple 16-step sequencer, allowing for rhythmic patterns to be easily programmed, and also supports shuffle for a more human-like feel. While it shares much of the architecture of the TR-707, the TR-727's focus on percussion made it particularly useful for Latin, jazz, and world music. Its sequencer is limited to 16 sounds, but it can trigger external MIDI gear for additional layers.
